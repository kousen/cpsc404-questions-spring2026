## Question

In this chapter, the author argues that conflict with your manager is inevitable because your incentives do not perfectly align. One common flashpoint is stolen credit. An employee does meaningful work, but the manager presents the success as their own, or at least takes the spotlight, in front of leadership. In many organizations, managers justify this as normal because they are expected to represent the team and collect wins for the department. But for employees, especially those who are not aiming for management, this can feel unfair and demotivating. The chapter suggests avoiding two extremes, silence or quitting, and instead using calm, private pushback to protect the working relationship while restoring balance.

## Context (optional)

Where should we draw the line between a manager representing the team’s work and a manager stealing credit?
